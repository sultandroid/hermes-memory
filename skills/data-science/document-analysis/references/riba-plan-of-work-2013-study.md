## 1. Overview & Purpose

The RIBA Plan of Work 2013 organises the process of **briefing, designing, constructing, maintaining, operating and using** building projects into **8 Work Stages (0–7)** and **8 Task Bars**. It is:

- A **process map and management tool** — not a contractual document
- Suitable for **all project sizes and sectors**
- Suitable for **all procurement routes** (traditional, design & build, management contracting, PFI)
- Integrates **sustainable design processes**
- Maps **Building Information Modelling (BIM)** processes
- Provides flexibility for **(town) planning procedures**

### Key shifts from RIBA Outline Plan of Work 2007

| 2007 (A–L) | 2013 (0–7) | Change |
|---|---|---|
| Stage A (Appraisal) | Stage 0 (Strategic Definition) | **New** — strategic appraisal before detailed brief |
| Stage A + B | Stage 1 (Preparation and Brief) | Merged |
| Stage C | Stage 2 (Concept Design) | Same |
| Stage D + part E | Stage 3 (Developed Design) | Coordinated design + cost alignment |
| Residual E | Stage 4 (Technical Design) | Includes specialist subcontractor design |
| Stage J + K | Stage 5 (Construction) | Mobilisation folded in |
| Stage L | Stage 6 (Handover and Close Out) | Broadly same |
| — | Stage 7 (In Use) | **New** — Post-occupancy Evaluation |
| Stages G, H, J (tendering) | → Moved to **Procurement task bar** | No standalone tendering stage |

## 2. The 8 Work Stages (0–7)

### Stage 0: Strategic Definition

| Task Bar | Tasks |
|---|---|
| **Core Objectives** | Identify client's Business Case and Strategic Brief and other core project requirements |
| **Procurement** | Initial considerations for assembling the project team |
| **Programme** | Establish Project Programme |
| **(Town) Planning** | Pre-application discussions may be required to test robustness of Strategic Brief |
| **Key Support Tasks** | Review Feedback from previous projects |
| **Sustainability** | Strategic sustainability review of client needs and potential sites, including reuse of existing facilities |
| **Information Exchange** | Strategic Brief |

**Contractor's perspective:** The contractor is typically **not yet involved** at Stage 0. However, this stage sets the strategic direction — the Business Case, budget, and site selection decisions that will determine procurement route and programme. For museum projects, this is where the decision between new-build, refurbishment, or extension of an existing museum is made.

---

### Stage 1: Preparation and Brief

| Task Bar | Tasks |
|---|---|
| **Core Objectives** | Develop Project Objectives, Quality Objectives, Project Outcomes, Sustainability Aspirations, Project Budget. Undertake Feasibility Studies and review Site Information |
| **Procurement** | Prepare Project Roles Table and Contractual Tree; continue assembling the project team |
| **Programme** | Review Project Programme |
| **(Town) Planning** | Pre-application discussions to determine suitability of Feasibility Studies |
| **Key Support Tasks** | Prepare Handover Strategy and Risk Assessments. Agree Schedule of Services, Design Responsibility Matrix, Information Exchanges, Project Execution Plan including Technology and Communication Strategies |
| **Sustainability** | Formal sustainability targets in Initial Project Brief; environmental requirements, building lifespan, future climate parameters |
| **Information Exchange** | Initial Project Brief |

**Contractor's perspective:** The RIBA Plan of Work 2013 **advocates establishing the project team during Stage 1**. This is the stage where a contractor may first become involved, particularly in two-stage design & build or management contracting routes. The **Project Roles Table** and **Contractual Tree** define who does what. The **Design Responsibility Matrix** is created at a strategic level — critical for understanding design liability boundaries.

**Museum projects:** Feasibility studies must consider environmental control requirements (humidity, temperature stability for artefacts), security and access logistics, visitor flow and public vs. back-of-house separation, collection storage and conservation needs.

---

### Stage 2: Concept Design

| Task Bar | Tasks |
|---|---|
| **Core Objectives** | Prepare Concept Design including outline proposals for structural design, building services systems, outline specifications, preliminary Cost Information, and relevant Project Strategies. Agree alterations to brief and issue **Final Project Brief** |
| **Procurement** | Activities depend on procurement route determined in Stage 1 |
| **Programme** | Review Project Programme |
| **(Town) Planning** | Planning applications **can** be submitted at end of Stage 2 (enhanced Concept Design), but this is not the norm |
| **Key Support Tasks** | Prepare Sustainability Strategy, Maintenance and Operational Strategy. Review Handover Strategy and Risk Assessments. Consider Construction Strategy including offsite fabrication. Develop Health and Safety Strategy |
| **Sustainability** | Formal sustainability pre-assessment; initial Building Regulations Part L assessment; environmental impact of key materials |
| **Information Exchange** | Concept Design including outline structural and building services design, associated Project Strategies, preliminary Cost Information, and **Final Project Brief** |

**Contractor's perspective:** The **Construction Strategy** is first considered here — buildability, offsite fabrication, logistics. The **Health and Safety Strategy** begins. For design & build procurement, the **Employer's Requirements** may be defined at this stage (or Stage 3, depending on variant). The **Change Control Procedures** are implemented after sign-off of Concept Design.

**Museum projects:** Concept Design must address large-span structural requirements for gallery spaces, environmental zoning (public areas vs. conservation-grade spaces), service routes that don't compromise gallery walls, and preliminary MEP strategy for humidity/temperature control.

---

### Stage 3: Developed Design

| Task Bar | Tasks |
|---|---|
| **Core Objectives** | Prepare Developed Design including **coordinated and updated** proposals for structural design, building services systems, outline specifications, Cost Information and Project Strategies |
| **Procurement** | Activities depend on procurement route |
| **Programme** | Can overlap with other stages depending on procurement route |
| **(Town) Planning** | **Recommended** stage for submitting planning applications |
| **Key Support Tasks** | Review and update all Project Strategies. Conclude Research and Development aspects. Implement Change Control Procedures. Review Construction and Health and Safety Strategies |
| **Sustainability** | Full formal sustainability assessment; interim Part L assessment; design stage carbon/energy declaration |
| **Information Exchange** | **Developed Design** including coordinated architectural, structural and building services design and **updated Cost Information** |

**Contractor's perspective:** This is the **critical coordination stage**. By the end of Stage 3: architectural, building services, and structural engineering designs are **spatially coordinated**; **Cost Information is aligned to Project Budget**; all Project Strategies are signed off. For **two-stage design & build**, the Employer's Requirements are typically defined at Stage 3. Specialist subcontractors may provide information/guidance at Stage 3 even though their design work happens at Stage 4.

**Museum projects:** Coordination is paramount — MEP services (humidity control, fire suppression, security systems) must be fully integrated with architectural intent. Gallery lighting, acoustic treatments, and display integration are developed. The Cost Information must reflect the high MEP and fit-out costs typical of museum projects.

---

### Stage 4: Technical Design

| Task Bar | Tasks |
|---|---|
| **Core Objectives** | Prepare Technical Design in accordance with Design Responsibility Matrix and Project Strategies — all architectural, structural, building services information, **specialist subcontractor design**, and specifications |
| **Procurement** | Activities depend on procurement route |
| **Programme** | Can overlap with other stages |
| **(Town) Planning** | Planning conditions addressed before work starts on site |
| **Key Support Tasks** | Prepare and submit Building Regulations submission. Review Construction Strategy including sequencing. Update Health and Safety Strategy |
| **Sustainability** | Formal sustainability assessment substantially complete; airtightness and continuity of insulation audited; Part L submission; non-technical user guide drafted |
| **Information Exchange** | **Completed Technical Design** of the project |

**Contractor's perspective:** This is the **contractor's most intensive design stage**: core design team's design work is **completed** (though they respond to Design Queries during Stage 5); **specialist subcontractor design** is developed and concluded (Performance Specified Work in JCT contracts); the **Design Responsibility Matrix** defines who designs what — critical for managing interfaces. Stages 4 and 5 often run **concurrently** for specialist subcontractor design aspects. For **one-stage design & build**, the Employer's Requirements are defined at Stage 4. **Fabrication drawings** are reviewed during Stage 4.

**Museum projects:** Specialist subcontractor design is extensive — museum-grade HVAC, fire suppression (gas-based systems for collection areas), security systems, audio-visual, display casework, lighting control systems. The Design Responsibility Matrix must clearly allocate these specialist packages.

---

### Stage 5: Construction

| Task Bar | Tasks |
|---|---|
| **Core Objectives** | Offsite manufacturing and onsite Construction in accordance with Construction Programme; resolution of Design Queries from site |
| **Procurement** | Administration of Building Contract including regular site inspections and review of progress |
| **Programme** | Can overlap with other stages |
| **(Town) Planning** | Contractor must comply with construction-specific planning conditions (e.g., noise monitoring) |
| **Key Support Tasks** | Implement Handover Strategy including commissioning, training, handover, asset management. Update Construction and Health and Safety Strategies. Compile 'As-constructed' Information |
| **Sustainability** | Design stage sustainability assessment certified; contractor's interim testing and monitoring reviewed; non-technical user guide complete; aftercare service set up |
| **Information Exchange** | **'As-constructed' Information** |

**Contractor's perspective:** This is the **contractor's lead stage**. Construction includes offsite manufactured components. Designer duties: respond to Design Queries (RFIs), carry out site inspections, produce quality reports. The **Handover Strategy** prepared in Stage 1 is now implemented. Commissioning, training, and asset management information are compiled. 'As-constructed' Information is produced (mixture of as-built from specialist subcontractors and final construction issue from design team).

**Museum projects:** Phased handover may be critical — galleries may be handed over in phases for fit-out while public areas continue construction. Environmental commissioning (humidity/temperature stabilization) must occur before collections are moved in. Security systems must be operational before artefact installation.

---

### Stage 6: Handover and Close Out

| Task Bar | Tasks |
|---|---|
| **Core Objectives** | Handover of building and conclusion of Building Contract |
| **Procurement** | Conclude administration of Building Contract |
| **Key Support Tasks** | Carry out activities in Handover Strategy including Feedback workshops. Fine tuning of building services. Updating of Project Information |
| **Sustainability** | Assistance with post-completion information for final sustainability certification |
| **Information Exchange** | Updated 'As-constructed' Information |

**Contractor's perspective:** Successful handover in line with Project Programme. Inspection and rectification of defects. Production of certification required by Building Contract. Feedback workshops to capture lessons learned. Commissioning and ensuring successful operation/management of the building.

**Museum projects:** The **Soft Landings** process (BSRIA) is particularly relevant — museum environmental systems need fine-tuning over an extended period. Training of museum facilities staff on complex HVAC, security, and fire systems is essential.

---

### Stage 7: In Use

| Task Bar | Tasks |
|---|---|
| **Core Objectives** | Undertake In Use services in accordance with Schedule of Services |
| **Key Support Tasks** | Conclude Handover Strategy activities including **Post-occupancy Evaluation**, review of Project Performance, Project Outcomes, and Research and Development aspects. Update Project Information in response to ongoing client Feedback |
| **Sustainability** | Observation of building operation in use; fine tuning and guidance for occupants; energy/carbon performance declared |
| **Information Exchange** | 'As-constructed' Information updated in response to ongoing client Feedback and maintenance/operational developments |

**Contractor's perspective:** This is a **new stage** in the 2013 edition. Contractors may have aftercare duties (defects liability period, commissioning follow-up). Post-occupancy evaluation feeds back into future projects. The end of a building's life feeds into Stage 0 of the follow-on project.

**Museum projects:** Post-occupancy evaluation is critical — verifying that environmental conditions meet conservation standards, visitor flow works as designed, and energy performance aligns with projections. Museum facilities management feedback loops into future gallery refurbishment projects.

## 4. Procurement Options

| # | Procurement Route | Key Characteristics | Contractor Involvement |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Traditional Contract** | Design complete before tender; client holds design risk | Contractor appointed at Stage 4/5 for construction only |
| 2 | **One-Stage Design & Build** (ER at Stage 3) | Single-stage tender; contractor responsible for design development from Stage 3 | Contractor appointed at end of Stage 3 |
| 3 | **Two-Stage Design & Build** (ER at Stage 4) | Two-stage tender; contractor appointed early (Stage 2/3) to assist with design | Contractor appointed early, works with design team |
| 4 | **Management Contract** | Contractor manages works packages; design team novated or retained | Contractor appointed at Stage 1/2 as management lead |
| 5 | **Contractor-Led Contract** | Contractor leads design and construction; design team novated | Contractor appointed at Stage 1 as project lead |
| 6 | **To Be Determined** | Holding option when procurement not yet finalised | — |

### Procurement Decision Factors for Museum Projects

- **Traditional:** Suitable when design is well-defined and client wants competitive fixed price. Risk: contractor has no input on buildability of complex museum systems.
- **Two-Stage Design & Build:** **Recommended for complex museum projects** — contractor's buildability input during Stage 2/3 is valuable for MEP-intensive, security-rich museum environments.
- **Management Contracting:** Suitable for very large museum projects with many specialist packages (AV, display, conservation systems).
- **Contractor-Led:** When the museum client wants single-point responsibility and the contractor has museum sector expertise.

## 6. Application to Museum Projects — Contractor's PM Perspective

### Key Considerations

1. **Environmental Control is Paramount** — Museum HVAC is highly specialised (stable 50% RH ±5%, 20°C ±2°C). Requires early contractor input on plant space, duct routes, and commissioning periods. The Construction Strategy must account for environmental stabilization periods before handover.

2. **Security and Collections Management** — Security systems (access control, CCTV, intruder detection) are specialist subcontractor packages. Must be defined in the Design Responsibility Matrix at Stage 1. Phased security commissioning is critical.

3. **Complex MEP and Fire Suppression** — Gas-based suppression (Inergen, Novec) in collection areas. Sprinklers in public areas only. Requires early specialist subcontractor involvement (Stage 3 input, Stage 4 design).

4. **Phased Handover (Stage 5 → 6)** — Galleries may be handed over in phases for fit-out. The Handover Strategy (prepared Stage 1) must address phased completion. Soft Landings process (BSRIA) is highly recommended.

5. **Large-Span Structures and Gallery Flexibility** — Structural design for column-free gallery spaces. Floor loading for heavy exhibits. Grid systems for flexible partition walls.

6. **Visitor Flow and Public Amenities** — Circulation, wayfinding, accessibility. Public vs. back-of-house separation. Retail, café, and event spaces.

7. **Post-Occupancy Evaluation (Stage 7)** — Verify environmental conditions meet conservation standards. Energy performance verification. Feedback into future gallery refurbishment projects.

### Recommended Procurement Route for Museum Projects

**Two-Stage Design & Build** is generally the most suitable for museum projects because:
- Contractor is appointed early (Stage 2/3) to provide buildability input
- Specialist subcontractors (HVAC, security, AV) can be engaged early
- Cost certainty is achieved through two-stage tendering
- The contractor's construction expertise informs the Developed Design (Stage 3)
- Phased handover can be planned from Stage 1

## 8. Key Takeaways for Contractors

1. **Get involved early** — The 2013 Plan encourages contractor appointment during Stage 1 (Preparation and Brief), especially for design & build and management contracting routes.
2. **The Design Responsibility Matrix is your bible** — It defines who designs what and when. Review it carefully at Stage 1 and ensure it captures all specialist subcontractor design packages.
3. **Construction Strategy starts at Stage 2** — Buildability, offsite fabrication, logistics, and supply chain considerations must be raised during Concept Design, not left until Stage 5.
4. **Stages 4 and 5 overlap** — Specialist subcontractor design continues into construction. The Design Programme must account for this.
5. **Handover Strategy is prepared at Stage 1** — Not at the end. Commissioning, training, phased handover, and Soft Landings are planned from the start.
6. **Stage 7 (In Use) is new** — Post-occupancy evaluation and aftercare services may be part of the contractor's scope. Define this in the Schedule of Services at Stage 1.
7. **BIM is embedded** — The Plan of Work maps to BIM processes. Information Exchanges align with the UK Government's Digital Plan of Work and COBie requirements.
8. **Sustainability is a continuous thread** — Sustainability Checkpoints run through all stages (0–7). The contractor must engage with sustainability certification (BREEAM, LEED) from Stage 5 onwards.
