# Client Delay / EOT Tracking — Contractor Position

## Why It Matters
For lump-sum Design & Build contracts (non-FIDIC), the contractor needs a clean paper trail for client-caused delays. Every late client input = potential Extension of Time (EOT) entitlement.

## EOT Notice Protocol (non-FIDIC)
Since the contract is **not** FIDIC-based:
1. **Do NOT use FIDIC Sub-Clause language** — reference the specific SOW clause instead
2. Send a **simple written notice** documenting:
   - What input is overdue
   - Which contract clause requires it
   - When it was originally required
   - The impact on schedule (which activities are blocked)
3. Follow up every 14 days until received
4. Escalate to next level (Project Director → Contracts) if no response after 28 days

## The Client Delay Tracker (Excel)
The baseline schedule Excel includes a "Client Delay Tracker" sheet with:
- **8 client-dependent items** with contractual trigger clauses
- **EOT Calculator** — auto-calculates delay days and EOT entitlement
- **Legal basis** — references contract-specific clauses (not FIDIC)
- **Notice log** — 6 pre-drafted notices to send via ACC/email

## Key Points
- The 14-day response period starts from the DATE THE LETTER IS SENT, not the internal date
- Hesham may send under a different document number (e.g., TQ instead of LET) — search Outlook by Arabic keywords
- Cumulative EOT grows every day inputs are late — update the tracker daily
- The EOT entitlement is against the design completion gate only — D300 (TOC) should remain protected via phased IFC release
